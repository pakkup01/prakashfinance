My Story

The long way around—
and why I'm glad I took it

I didn't start out thinking I'd be in banking. I fell into it through a high-volume transaction role where the goal was speed—get them in, get them out. Move the queue. Hit the numbers. And I was good at it. But something nagged at me: the customers who stayed a little longer, the ones who wanted to talk, were the ones who left feeling genuinely better about their money. The ones we rushed through? Most of them came back confused, frustrated, or with a product they didn't fully understand.

That stuck with me.

"When you take the time to actually teach someone, they trust you. And when they trust you, they come back."

At NMB Bank, I saw a chance to test that instinct. I started leading digital banking education sessions—not formal classes, just sitting with customers, walking them through the tools, answering the questions they were embarrassed to ask at the counter. Adoption rates climbed. Branch pressure dropped. And I saw something I hadn't expected: customers who felt capable were also customers who were confident. They stopped feeling like they were at the mercy of the bank. That shift mattered to me.

Now at LendDirect, I work in a fast-paced environment built around sales targets. And I'll be honest—that tension between sales and service is real. But I've never been comfortable pushing products on people who can't afford them. So I reframed it: I focus on understanding the situation first. Why are they borrowing? What can they realistically repay? What are their actual goals? The sales follow naturally when the recommendation is right. I handle 50+ customers a day, make proactive outreach calls, and I've learned that targets aren't something to fear—they're just a measure of how well you're actually serving people.

I'm ready for the next step. I want to move beyond short-term lending into comprehensive financial advice. I want to sit across from someone and help them think through their whole picture—their savings, their investments, their retirement, their goals. I want to work in an environment where the relationship outlasts the transaction. That's the kind of banker I want to be.

What I Bring

Strengths, with the stories behind them

These aren't buzzwords from a resume. Each one comes from a real situation—a real customer, a real moment where I had to choose between what was easy and what was right.

🗣️
Customer Conversations

A nervous borrower came in once, clearly overwhelmed. She'd read something online about interest rates and worked herself into a panic before she even sat down. Instead of jumping into the application, I just asked her what she was worried about. Twenty minutes later, she had a clear picture of what she could afford, what her options were, and a plan that actually made sense for her life.

She came back three months later—not because she needed another loan, but because she wanted to talk through her savings plan.
🎯
Needs-Based Advising

People often ask for one thing when they actually need something different. Someone asks for a top-up loan to cover a bill—but when I dig a little, I find out they've got three open credit products they're juggling. The right answer isn't always the one they asked for. I ask questions first. Then I recommend.

The best financial advice sounds less like a pitch and more like a conversation where someone finally feels heard.
🛡️
Compliance Mindset

I've worked in environments where cutting corners was quietly normalized. Quick approvals, skipped steps, assumptions in place of verification. I've always pushed back on that—not because I'm rigid, but because I've seen what happens to customers when the process fails them. Compliance isn't paperwork. It's protection.

I think of every KYC check, every suitability question, as a moment where I'm doing right by the customer—not just the institution.
📈
Sales Targets

Targets don't stress me out. What would stress me out is hitting them the wrong way. When I focus on genuinely understanding what someone needs and recommending the right product—not the highest-margin one—the numbers take care of themselves. Service-first isn't a philosophy I perform. It's how I actually work.

Some of my highest-conversion days have been the ones where I said "no" the most—because those nos built trust that turned into yes later.
🤝
Relationship Building

I follow up. Not because it's in the script, but because I'm genuinely curious how things went for someone. A quick check-in after a loan approval. A note when someone mentioned they were going through something hard. Those small moments compound. Over time, they turn a transaction into a relationship— and a customer into someone who refers their family.

The goal isn't to close a deal. The goal is to be the person they call when money gets complicated.
Why Banking

Where I want to do this work—and why

The Shift I Believe In

The financial industry is moving—slowly, but undeniably—from transactional to relationship-based. The branches that will thrive aren't the ones that process the fastest. They're the ones that customers trust the most. I want to be part of that shift, not waiting for it to happen around me.

The Full Picture

What excites me about advisory roles is the scope. Right now I touch one piece of someone's financial life—a loan, a line of credit. I want to help people think about the whole thing: their savings, their investments, their insurance needs, their retirement. That's where advice becomes genuinely valuable.

Trust as Infrastructure

Banks run on capital. But the thing that actually makes them function—the thing that keeps people coming back—is trust. I take that seriously. Every interaction I have is either building trust or eroding it. I want to work in an environment that takes that as seriously as I do.

The Environment I'm Looking For

Professionalism. Long-term thinking. A culture where doing right by the customer is never in conflict with doing right by the business—because the best institutions know those are the same thing. I'm looking for a team that holds itself to that standard.

"I'm not looking for a job that lets me sell financial products. I'm looking for a role where I can build something that matters—where the relationship I have with a customer at 30 is still serving them well at 60. That's the work I want to do. That's the kind of advisor I'm building toward."

My Approach

How I actually work

Not a methodology. Not a framework. Just an honest look at the principles I bring to every customer interaction.

1

Listen Before You Speak

The fastest way to waste someone's time is to start talking before you understand what they actually need. I ask questions. I listen for what's underneath the question—the anxiety, the goal, the misunderstanding—and I work from there. The conversation might take a bit longer. The outcome is almost always better.

2

Balancing Sales with Responsibility

I hold both at once. Sales targets are real, and I take them seriously. But I won't put someone in a product that doesn't fit their situation—not because I'm nervous about compliance, but because it's genuinely wrong. The good news? When you do it right, the two things rarely conflict.

3

Repayment Capacity First

Before I recommend anything, I think about whether this person can actually sustain it. Not just can they qualify—can they afford it without it becoming a burden six months from now? That kind of thinking isn't just ethical; it reduces defaults, builds long-term relationships, and protects the institution.

4

Handling Pressure Without Losing Judgment

I've worked in environments where the pressure to approve, to upsell, to close—can cloud your judgment. I've learned to stay grounded in busy moments. When I'm handling 50+ customers in a day, I remind myself that each one of those people is making a real financial decision. That keeps me sharp.

5

The Long Game

I think in relationships, not transactions. A customer who feels well-served today might not need anything else from me for a year. But they'll remember how that conversation went. And when they're ready to buy a home, start investing, or plan for retirement—I want to be the person they call. That's the only version of this job that interests me.
